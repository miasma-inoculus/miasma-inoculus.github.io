THE THIRTEENTH KNOCK

She had always been meticulous about locks. Twelve turns of the key. A ritual as old as her tenure in the Ashford estate.

On the night of the twenty-third, she heard knocking. One. Two. Three. She set down her candle and listened. Four. Five. Six. The knocks were deliberate. Seven. Eight. Nine. Ten. Eleven. Twelve.

She opened the door. The fog lay thick across the moor. There was no carriage, no figure, no footprint in the frost. She was about to close the door when she heard it.

The thirteenth knock.

It came from behind her. From inside the house. She turned slowly. The portraits of the Ashfords stared forward. All except one. The portrait of Edmund Ashford, dead these forty years, had turned its gaze toward the open cellar door.

THE LAST PASSENGER

The Blackwood Line was notorious for fog that swallowed sound. Elias, the ticket collector, walked the length of the train. Every carriage was a ghost of velvet and mahogany.

In the final car sat a man in a formal suit that smelled of damp earth and coal soot. Elias reached out. "Ticket, please." The man didn't look up, but extended a pale hand holding a slip of card.

A date from the disaster.

The ticket was dated 1847—the night the trestle gave way. Elias looked at the passenger's face, but there was only a smooth, featureless surface under the brim of the hat. Outside, the train didn't emerge from the tunnel. It just kept descending into the dark.

THE CARTOGRAPHER'S WIFE

Thomas Thorne mapped the Uncharted Coast, but the coast was mapping him back. Elena watched as his inkwells turned thick and dark, smelling not of iron gall, but of copper and salt.

He began to draw lines that didn't follow geography, but anatomy. "The borders are bleeding, Elena," he whispered. One night, he vanished. Elena found his last map on the desk.

Mapped into existence.

The map showed their house, their room, and her—standing at the desk. As she touched the ink, her fingers felt like parchment. She looked out the window and saw only the white void of an unpainted page.

THE MIRROR AT THE END

Silas bought the silver-framed mirror because the reflection seemed to lag behind by a fraction of a second. It was a curiosity until the lag became a minute, then an hour.

He watched his reflection live a different life. While Silas grew grey and gaunt with dread, the Silas in the mirror remained young, vibrant, and smiling. One evening, the reflection reached out and touched the glass from the inside.

The swap of souls.

Silas felt a sudden, crushing cold. He looked down at his hands and saw they were made of silvered glass. Outside the mirror, the young man adjusted his tie, blew out the candle, and walked out of the room, leaving Silas trapped in the dark of the hallway glass.

THE WATCHMAN'S HOUR

Old Arthur knew the house had secrets, but he didn't know it was hungry. His task was simple: keep the clocks wound. If they stopped, the house stopped breathing.

But at midnight, the pendulum of the great hall clock swung twice as wide. The shadows on the wall stretched out like claws. Arthur felt the air grow heavy as the gears groaned.

The phantom thirteenth chime.

When the clock struck thirteen, the hallway stretched into infinity. Arthur looked into the clock's face and saw his own childhood staring back. He realized he wasn't the watchman—he was the fuel. Every tick of the clock was a second of his life being consumed to keep the house standing.

THE GOVERNESS' LETTER

The letter was sealed with black wax. Miss Penhaligon found it tucked into the vanity. It was a warning from the woman she replaced, but the handwriting was disturbingly familiar.

The children were too quiet. They sat in the nursery, playing with dolls that looked exactly like the staff. "The Master is coming," they whispered in unison. Miss Penhaligon opened the letter to find it was a script.

Instructions for the new arrival.

She turned the page and saw her own death described in perfect, rhythmic prose. She looked up and saw the children standing in the doorway, holding the needle and thread mentioned in the letter's final paragraph.
